# Purpose

The Causality Framework is responsible for ensuring that every meaningful change within Project Draugr has a logical cause and produces appropriate consequences.

It transforms isolated simulation updates into an interconnected chain of events that collectively form a believable reality.

Rather than scripting outcomes, the framework evaluates relationships between systems and propagates consequences throughout the world.

Every significant event must be explainable through preceding conditions.

---

# Design Philosophy

Reality is not a collection of isolated events.

Reality is an interconnected web of causes and consequences.

A drought may cause famine.

A famine may trigger migration.

Migration may increase political tension.

Political tension may lead to war.

War may collapse civilizations.

Collapsed civilizations may create forgotten knowledge.

Forgotten knowledge may become future discoveries.

Every event becomes the cause of future events.

---

# Core Principles

## Every Effect Requires a Cause

No significant world change should occur without an identifiable origin.

---

## Every Cause Produces Consequences

Actions should propagate through the simulation according to the systems they influence.

---

## Consequences Should Scale Naturally

Small actions create small consequences.

Large actions may reshape civilizations.

---

## Multiple Causes May Produce One Outcome

Reality is rarely influenced by a single factor.

The framework evaluates combined influences before producing results.

---

## Multiple Outcomes May Emerge From One Cause

One event may simultaneously affect:

- ecosystems
- economies
- civilizations
- relationships
- history

---

# Responsibilities

The Causality Framework is responsible for:

- evaluating state changes
- identifying causes
- determining consequences
- propagating world effects
- resolving dependency chains
- generating emergent events
- preventing illogical outcomes
- maintaining simulation continuity

It does not simulate the world.

It interprets simulation changes.

- seasonal change

---

## Biological Causes

Generated by living systems.

Examples:

- disease

- mutation
- migration

---

Generated by a single conscious entity.

Examples:

- invention
- exploration
- diplomacy
- betrayal

---

## Collective Causes

Generated by groups.

Examples:

- revolutions
- wars
- religious movements
- trade alliances
- scientific collaboration

---

## Systemic Causes

Generated by long-term simulation pressure.

Examples:

- inflation
- economic collapse
- overpopulation

# Chain Reactions

One consequence may generate additional causes.

Example:

```
Meteor Strike

↓

Forest Fire

↓

Wildlife Migration

↓

Food Shortage

↓

Settlement Expansion

↓

Territorial Conflict

↓

War

↓

↓

```

Chain reactions continue until no further meaningful consequences remain.

---

# Causal Weight

Every cause carries a weight representing its influence.

Factors include:

- severity
- duration
- geographic scope
- affected population
- historical importance

Higher causal weight results in broader propagation.

---

# Probability and Uncertainty

Not every cause guarantees the same outcome.

The framework supports probabilistic consequences.

Example:

```
Poor Harvest

↓

80% chance of food shortage

↓

40% chance of migration

↓

15% chance of rebellion

↓

5% chance of societal collapse
```

Probability is influenced by current world conditions.

---

# Feedback Loops

Some consequences reinforce their original causes.

Example:

```
Economic Recession

↓

Reduced Production

↓

Lower Employment

↓

Reduced Spending

↓

Deeper Recession
```

The framework identifies positive and negative feedback loops.

---

# Cross-Domain Propagation

Consequences may move between simulation domains.

Example:

```
Environment

↓

Agriculture

↓

Economy

↓

Politics

↓

↓

```

---

# Event Thresholds

Minor changes accumulate until thresholds are reached.

Example:

```
Citizen Dissatisfaction

12%

18%

24%

31%

48%

↓

Rebellion Triggered
```

Thresholds prevent excessive event generation.

---

# Event Consolidation

When multiple related causes occur simultaneously, they may be merged into a larger event.

Example:

```
Food Shortage

+

Political Corruption

+

Economic Decline

↓

National Crisis
```

This reduces event fragmentation while improving narrative coherence.

---

# Causal Priority

When multiple causes compete, priority is determined by:

1. Reality Rules
2. Simulation State
3. Historical Context
4. Causal Weight
5. Random Variance (if applicable)

# Performance Considerations

The framework evaluates only meaningful state changes.

Minor fluctuations below configurable thresholds may be ignored.

Propagation depth may be limited based on:

- simulation fidelity
- available resources
- importance of affected entities

---

# Developer Notes

The Causality Framework should never contain scripted stories.

It produces logical consequences.

Narratives emerge later from those consequences.

Maintaining the separation between causality and storytelling ensures that reality remains authoritative and believable.
