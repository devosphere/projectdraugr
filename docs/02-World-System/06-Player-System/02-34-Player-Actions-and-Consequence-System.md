# Purpose

This document defines how player actions influence the world.

It establishes:

- consequences;
- reactions;
- delayed effects;
- social impact;
- environmental impact;
- historical impact.

---

# Design Philosophy

The player does not control the world.

The player interacts with it.

---

# Core Principle

## Every Action Creates A Reaction.

The world responds to behavior.

Not intention.

---

# No Alignment System

Project Draugr does not use traditional:

- good;
- evil;
- morality meters.

---

# Reason

Actions can have different interpretations.

A decision may:

- help one group;
- harm another;
- create unexpected results.

---

# Example

A player destroys a monster nest.

Possible outcomes:

Positive:

- protects nearby settlements.

Negative:

- removes a food source for another creature population.

---

# Action Categories

Actions are classified as:

```
Personal

↓

Social

↓

Environmental

↓

Historical
```

---

# Personal Actions

Effects on the player's own life.

Examples:

- training;
- exploration;
- risk taking;
- preparation.

---

# Social Actions

Effects on individuals.

Examples:

- helping;
- betraying;
- teaching;
- negotiating.

---

# Environmental Actions

Effects on nature.

Examples:

- hunting;
- harvesting;
- construction;
- destruction.

---

# Historical Actions

Effects that influence future generations.

Examples:

- founding settlements;
- discoveries;
- wars;
- cultural changes.

---

# Consequence Timing

Consequences occur at different speeds.

---

# Immediate Consequences

Effects happen instantly.

Examples:

- injury;
- combat result;
- broken equipment.

---

# Short-Term Consequences

Effects happen after hours or days.

Examples:

- reputation changes;
- resource shortages;
- relationships.

---

# Long-Term Consequences

Effects happen after months or years.

Examples:

- civilization changes;
- historical reputation;
- environmental transformation.

---

# Hidden Consequences

Not all consequences are visible.

The player may not immediately know:

- who was affected;
- what changed;
- what was triggered.

---

# Reason

The player experiences the world from limited perspective.

The player is not the Overseer.

---

# Decision Philosophy

The system evaluates:

- action;
- context;
- result.

---

# Context Matters

The same action may create different outcomes.

Example:

Taking food.

Scenario 1:

Taking abandoned food.

Result:

Little impact.

Scenario 2:

Taking a family's only food supply.

Result:

Severe social consequence.

---

# NPC Reactions

NPCs react based on:

- personal experience;
- culture;
- relationship;
- information received.

---

# Civilization Reactions

Civilizations may respond through:

- diplomacy;
- trade changes;
- laws;
- conflict.

---

# Reputation Consequences

Actions influence reputation.

Examples:

Helping travelers:

May create trust.

Destroying property:

May create hostility.

---

# Relationship Consequences

Individual relationships evolve.

A single action may:

- create friendship;
- destroy trust;
- create fear.

---

# Environmental Consequences

The world changes through player activity.

Examples:

Deforestation:

May affect ecosystems.

Overhunting:

May reduce populations.

Construction:

May transform landscapes.

---

# Economic Consequences

Player actions influence resources.

Examples:

Creating a trade route:

May grow settlements.

Destroying resources:

May create scarcity.

---

# Knowledge Consequences

Discoveries may spread.

Examples:

A new farming method may:

- improve civilization;
- change traditions;
- create competition.

---

# Historical Consequences

Major actions become part of world history.

Examples:

- founding cities;
- defeating threats;
- causing disasters.

---

# Player Accountability

The world does not forget meaningful actions.

A player cannot simply:

- leave;
- reload;
- ignore consequences.

---

# Chronicle Impact

Actions become part of the Chronicle.

The Chronicle records:

- choices;
- achievements;
- failures.

---

# Legacy Impact

After death, consequences continue.

Examples:

A settlement survives because of the player.

A conflict continues because of the player.

A discovery changes future generations.

---

# The Overseer's Observation

The Overseer evaluates:

- choices;
- reasoning;
- adaptation.

Not just results.

---

# The Overseer's Perspective

The Overseer understands:

- hidden causes;
- unseen consequences;
- complete context.

---

# Player Freedom

The player may choose any path.

The system does not force:

- heroism;
- villainy;
- neutrality.

---

# Integration With Other Systems

## World Continuity System

Preserves consequences.

---

## Reputation System

Tracks social perception.

---

## Civilization System

Tracks societal effects.

---

## Chronicle System

Records personal history.

---

# Developer Notes

Consequences should feel:

- logical;
- fair;
- understandable.

Avoid arbitrary punishment.

---

# Edge Cases

## Player Did Not Know Consequence

The world may still react.

Lack of knowledge does not remove impact.

---

## Player Makes A Mistake

Mistakes create stories.

---

## Player Attempts To Manipulate Reputation

Actions matter more than public claims.

---

# Future Expansion

Potential future systems:

- dynamic quest generation;
- historical simulation;
- political consequences;
- generational conflicts.

---

# Final Statement

The Awakened Soul does not write the world.

They contribute to its story.

Every choice becomes a thread.

Every action creates movement.

Every Chronicle leaves evidence that someone was here.
