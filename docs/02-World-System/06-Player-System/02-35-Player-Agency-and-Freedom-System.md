# Purpose

This document defines player freedom and agency within Project Draugr.

It establishes:

- player autonomy;
- available life paths;
- world response;
- limitations of freedom;
- emergent gameplay principles.

---

# Design Philosophy

Freedom does not mean the world obeys the player.

Freedom means the player can make meaningful choices inside a consistent world.

---

# Core Principle

## The Player Chooses Their Path. The World Determines The Result.

The player controls decisions.

The universe controls consequences.

---

# Definition Of Player Agency

Player agency is the ability to:

- make meaningful decisions;
- pursue personal goals;
- influence the world;
- create unique experiences.

---

# True Freedom

Project Draugr allows players to decide:

- who they become;
- where they live;
- what they value;
- what risks they take;
- what legacy they leave.

---

# No Required Destiny

The Awakened Soul is not required to:

- save the world;
- become powerful;
- discover all mysteries;
- participate in conflicts.

---

---

# No Correct Way To Play

Every Chronicle is valid.

A small peaceful life may be as meaningful as a world-changing one.

---

# Player Goals

The player creates personal objectives.

Examples:

"I want to build a permanent home."

"I want to discover every region."

"I want to create a civilization."

"I want to understand the Overseer."

"I want to survive one year."

---

# Emergent Gameplay

The game does not need to predict every story.

Stories emerge from:

- player decisions;
- world simulation;
- NPC behavior;
- consequences.

---

# Example Emergent Story

A player:

1. Builds a small farm.
2. Helps nearby travelers.
3. Gains reputation.
4. Attracts settlers.
5. Creates a village.
6. Becomes part of regional history.

No quest required.

The world created the story.

---

# World Independence

The world continues without player involvement.

Civilizations:

- grow;
- fight;
- trade;
- change.

Nature:

- evolves;
- adapts;
- survives.

---

# Player Importance

The player can become important.

But importance must be earned.

---

# The World Does Not Wait

Events continue without the player.

Examples:

- wars happen;
- settlements change;
- creatures migrate;
- discoveries occur.

---

# Freedom Limitations

Freedom exists within reality.

The player cannot:

- ignore physics;
- avoid consequences;
- instantly master skills;
- bypass survival.

---

# Why Limitations Create Freedom

Without rules:

Actions have no meaning.

Without consequences:

Choices have no weight.

---

# Player Creativity

Players are encouraged to solve problems differently.

Example:

A player needs shelter.

Possible solutions:

- build a house;
- join a settlement;
- create a cave shelter;
- travel to a safer region.

---

# Multiple Solutions

The world should support:

- different strategies;
- different lifestyles;
- different outcomes.

---

# Quest Philosophy

Project Draugr does not rely primarily on traditional quests.

Instead:

The world creates situations.

The player creates responses.

---

# Dynamic Events

Events may emerge from:

- civilization changes;
- environmental conditions;
- player actions;
- world simulation.

---

# Player And The Overseer

The Overseer observes freedom.

The test is not:

"Did the player follow the intended path?"

The question is:

"What did the player choose when given freedom?"

---

# Chronicle Uniqueness

Each Chronicle should feel different.

Differences come from:

- starting conditions;
- decisions;
- relationships;
- discoveries;
- consequences.

# Final Statement

The world does not exist to give the player a story.

The world exists.

The player enters it.

Their choices create a story.

Some become forgotten lives.

But every Chronicle belongs to the person who lived it.
