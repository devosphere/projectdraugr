# 08 – Geography

> **Project Draugr**
>
> *The world existed before it was explored.*

---

# Purpose

This document defines the geographical structure of Project Draugr.

It establishes the permanent physical layout of the world and the environmental rules governing its terrain.

Geography forms the foundation upon which every ecosystem, civilization, and Chronicle exists.

---

# Design Philosophy

The geography of Project Draugr is permanent.

Mountains do not relocate.

Rivers do not suddenly change course without natural causes.

Forests remain where nature placed them.

Players may alter the world's state, but they do not redefine its geography.

The land remembers.

---

# The World

Project Draugr contains one canonical world.

The world is finite.

Its exact size is intentionally undefined during the blueprint phase.

The world may expand through future content updates, but existing geography should never be regenerated or reset.

---

# Regions

The world is divided into geographical regions.

A region represents a naturally distinct area of the world.

Each region possesses unique:

- terrain
- climate
- ecology
- resources
- wildlife
- monster populations
- environmental hazards

Regions are the primary organizational unit of the world simulation.

---

# MVP Scope

The Minimum Viable Product begins with **one persistent region**.

This region should be sufficiently large to support meaningful exploration, settlement, and long-term gameplay.

Future regions expand the world without replacing existing ones.

---

# Terrain Types

The world may contain combinations of the following terrain.

## Forest

Dense vegetation supporting abundant wildlife and natural resources.

---

## Plains

Open landscapes suitable for travel, farming, and large settlements.

---

## Mountains

High elevations containing valuable minerals, caves, and difficult travel routes.

---

## Hills

Rolling terrain that connects plains and mountains.

---

## Rivers

Natural freshwater systems.

Provide:

- drinking water
- fishing
- transportation
- fertile land

---

## Streams

Smaller freshwater sources feeding larger rivers.

Important for early survival.

---

## Lakes

Stable freshwater bodies supporting diverse ecosystems.

---

## Swamps

Wetlands characterized by difficult movement, abundant vegetation, and unique wildlife.

---

## Coastlines

Transition zones between land and ocean.

Support fishing and maritime exploration.

---

## Oceans

The largest bodies of water.

Future updates may introduce oceanic exploration.

---

## Caves

Natural underground formations.

May contain:

- minerals
- underground ecosystems
- ancient ruins
- monsters

---

## Ancient Ruins

Permanent remnants of forgotten civilizations.

Ruins are part of the geography.

They are not randomly generated dungeons.

---

# Elevation

Terrain elevation influences:

- temperature
- weather
- visibility
- wildlife
- vegetation
- travel difficulty
- construction suitability

Higher elevations generally become harsher environments.

---

# Water Systems

Freshwater naturally flows through the world.

Examples include:

- rivers
- streams
- lakes
- springs

Water systems support:

- ecosystems
- agriculture
- wildlife
- human settlements

Water should behave consistently with natural geography.

---

# Natural Resources

Different geographical features contain different resources.

Examples include:

Mountains

- ore
- stone
- caves

Forests

- wood
- plants
- wildlife

Rivers

- fish
- freshwater
- fertile soil

Swamps

- medicinal plants
- reeds
- unique wildlife

Resources should encourage exploration rather than uniform distribution.

---

# Settlement Suitability

Not every location is suitable for construction.

Players should naturally learn to evaluate terrain.

Examples of desirable locations include:

- stable ground
- nearby freshwater
- access to resources
- natural visibility
- defensible positions

Examples of poor locations include:

- flood-prone land
- unstable slopes
- exposed cliffs
- dense monster territories
- seasonal wetlands

The world rewards observation rather than arbitrary building restrictions.

---

# Travel

Geography determines travel difficulty.

Examples:

Dense forests slow movement.

Mountains restrict travel.

Rivers create natural barriers.

Roads improve movement only after players construct them.

Travel should feel shaped by the land itself.

---

# Exploration

Every location is explorable.

No region exists purely for decoration.

Every area should possess potential value through:

- resources
- ecology
- history
- danger
- discovery

Exploration should be driven by curiosity rather than quest markers.

---

# World Expansion

Future updates may introduce additional regions.

New regions should:

- expand the world's borders
- preserve existing geography
- integrate naturally with the persistent simulation

Previously explored regions must never be regenerated.

---

# Design Principles

The geography of Project Draugr should always satisfy the following.

## Permanent

The land itself does not reset.

---

## Natural

Terrain follows believable geological patterns.

---

## Meaningful

Every geographical feature influences gameplay.

---

## Discoverable

Players learn the world through exploration rather than map completion objectives.

---

## Persistent

Every Chronicle explores the same physical world.

---

# Final Principle

The geography of Project Draugr is not a level.

It is not a playground.

It is an ancient world that simply exists.

Players do not unlock it.

They slowly come to understand it.

The land remains long after every Chronicle has ended.
